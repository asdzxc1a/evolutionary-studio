# THE PURRING CITY

### A Feature Animation Synopsis

---

## OPENING: THE WORLD THAT PURRS

We open on darkness. Then — a low rumble. Not thunder. Something alive. A frequency that moves through stone, through wood, through the sleeping body of MARKO SHEVCHUK (28), who lies in a hammock, eyes closed, one hand draped over the side. His fingers rest on the floorboards, which vibrate gently. This is how Murchyk says good night.

DAWN. Marko wakes, rolls out of the hammock, steps onto the vibrating floor. He walks to the window. Below: a medieval city built on seven hills connected by stone bridges, ramps wide enough for wagons, and elevated walkways. And everywhere — cats. Not house cats. Horse-sized guardian cats. Sleeping on bridges, grooming on rooftops, sitting in intersections and staring at absolutely nothing.

Marko pulls on his keeper's harness — stiff leather across the chest, loops for tools, a pouch of dried fish — and climbs down his building's exterior ladder (stairs are a luxury for people who don't wake at 4 AM). He reaches the street and walks past a GIANT TABBY sleeping on the Central Bridge. Morning traffic has already rerouted. A merchant pulls his fruit cart under the cat's belly, between its legs. The cat's tail twitches in its sleep and launches the cart into a canal. The merchant stands at the water's edge, watching his cabbages float away. "Third time this moon." He doesn't even sound angry. Just tired.

Marko arrives at the GROOMING STATION: imagine a car wash, but medieval, and for animals that do not want to be washed. Giant rotating brushes on poles. Water troughs the size of canals. Fur-drying bellows operated by teams of four. A GREY GUARDIAN is mid-wash. Marko gives the order to rinse. The cat shakes. A wall of water hits the entire adjacent street. Pedestrians don't flinch. They just ring out their hats. A TOURIST screams. Everyone stares at the tourist.

This is Murchyk. A city that works — not because it's efficient, but because every human in it has spent their entire life learning when to yield, when to wait, and when to simply accept that a six-hundred-pound animal is going to sit on your silk display and there is nothing you can do about it.

Marko is good at his job. Too good. He speaks to cats the way other men speak to children — low voice, slow hands, never from behind. His own guardian, VOVK, a massive dark-orange tom with a notch in his left ear, waits for him at the end of every shift, headbutting him so hard Marko slides three feet. It's affection. It also means Marko goes through two pairs of boots a month.

He has no close friends. Keepers don't. You partner with your cat. Other humans are just... the people you nod to at the fish market.

ANCIENT KEEPER PANAS (70s), the only person Marko talks to voluntarily, sits outside the station mending harnesses. Panas watches the morning Dead Stop — every cat in the city simultaneously freezes and stares at the same invisible point for thirty seconds. Traffic halts. A child tugs his mother's hand: "What are they looking at?" Mother: "Nobody knows. Don't ask." The cats unfreeze. Life resumes.

Panas, not looking up from his stitching: "They're listening to a frequency we can't hear. Or they're idiots. After forty years, I'm genuinely unsure."

---

## THE GIRL WITH THE WHITE CAT

A commotion at the Western Gate. Marko hears it from the station — not screaming, but the particular silence that happens when a crowd sees something they don't understand.

At the gate: a line of REFUGEES from the east. Skelya has fallen. The empire rolled cages through the streets, captured the guardian cats, muzzled them, loaded them onto iron wagons. Without the purr, the resonant stone cracked. Buildings fell. The city died in three days.

The refugees are processed. Some have nothing. Some carry bundles. None have cats.

Except one.

KALYNA BONDAR (24) walks through the gate with a WHITE CAT. Not a guardian — smaller, wilder, with pale blue eyes and frost on her whiskers. BILA. She presses against Kalyna's leg, ears flat, tail low, every muscle coiled.

The processing keepers try to approach for the standard bonding assessment. Bila HISSES — a sound that sends two keepers stumbling backward and makes three nearby guardians raise their heads from their naps. A white cat that hisses like a guardian. Unbonded to a district. Bonded to a person. This doesn't happen.

Marko watches from across the square. He reads Bila's body language the way a sailor reads a sky. Flattened ears: fear, not aggression. Low tail: she's protecting Kalyna, not threatening the keepers. Wide pupils: she's processing too much input. She needs space and dark and quiet, and she's getting a crowd and daylight and noise.

He WANTS to walk over. He WANTS to tell the processing keepers they're doing it wrong, they're crowding her, you never approach an unbonded cat from the front —

Instead, he walks past. Doesn't look at Kalyna. Mutters:

"Your harness stitching is wrong. The buckle faces outward so the cat can't chew it loose."

Kalyna, exhausted, filthy, holding the only thing she has left: "I didn't ask."

Marko, already walking away: "The leather's also wet. It'll rot by Thursday."

---

## FIRST NIGHT

Kalyna is assigned temporary quarters in the Eastern District. It's a small room with a bed, a window, and a water basin. Bila squeezes in beside her, taking up most of the floor.

10 PM. The PURR-CURFEW begins.

Every guardian in the city begins purring simultaneously. The frequency is subsonic — you feel it before you hear it. The walls vibrate. The floor vibrates. The bed vibrates. Kalyna, who has not slept in a real bed since Skelya fell, closes her eyes.

And wakes up on the floor.

The bed has shaken itself sideways. The basin has migrated to the opposite wall. A cup has rattled off the table and shattered.

Morning. Marko sees her at the processing station, rubbing her back.

"Hammock."

"What?"

"You need a hammock."

He walks away. That night, she finds a hammock hanging in her room. No note. No instruction. The knots are keeper knots — complex, precise, and clearly tied by someone who does this every day.

Bila watches Kalyna climb into the hammock. Then Bila lies on the floor directly underneath it, so that if it falls, she's there. The purr begins. The hammock sways gently. Kalyna sleeps through the night for the first time in weeks.

---

## THE COMEDY OF COHABITATION

Kalyna's first week in Murchyk is a series of revelations, each one absurd.

She discovers the FISH MARKET, run by OLEK, the most powerful man in the city — not because of politics, but because he controls the mackerel supply. Olek has two bodyguards. Not for himself. For the fish. When Olek adjusts the price by one coin, the city's economy shifts. Cats follow his cart through the streets like a parade. Kalyna: "Why does everyone bow to the fish man?" Marko: "You bow too. Unless you want your guardian to follow HIS cart instead of you."

She witnesses her first KNEADING INCIDENT. A giant orange tom, deeply content after a belly rub from his keeper, kneads the cobblestones of the market square, leaving claw marks two inches deep. The claw marks are officially classified as "Blessings of Contentment." A silk merchant, staring at his shredded inventory: "Blessed. I am so blessed." Marko: "Insurance covers Blessings." Merchant: "Insurance STOPPED covering Blessings after the seventh claim this quarter."

She attends her first CAT COUNCIL meeting. The eldest guardian, SIRKO, votes by sitting on one side of a weighted platform: left for YES, right for NO. But Sirko sits in the MIDDLE. The council secretary flips through a massive binder: "Precedent 4.7: the Middle Sit. Interpretations range from 'abstention' to 'contempt for the question itself.' Fourteen academic papers have been published. No consensus." Sirko yawns. The council adjourns.

And then: 3 AM, her fifth night. A SOUND like war drums. The floor shakes. Kalyna grabs a kitchen knife and runs outside in her nightclothes, certain the empire has arrived.

She finds Marko sitting on his stoop, drinking tea, watching fifteen horse-sized cats sprint in circles through the streets, skidding around corners, launching off ramps, knocking over empty market stalls.

NIGHT ZOOMIES.

Bila rockets past them both, ears pinned, legs a blur.

Marko doesn't spill his tea. "It stops in about ten minutes."

Kalyna stands there, knife in hand, breathing hard. A calico guardian thunders past and clips a lamppost. The lamppost bends. The calico doesn't slow down.

"...Does this happen every night?"

"Mostly. Worse during a full moon."

Kalyna looks at the knife in her hand. At the tea in his. She sits down next to him. Not close. But near. They watch the cats run.

---

## COUNCILOR RUDA

RUDA LYSENKO (52) runs the city's infrastructure the way a surgeon runs an operating room: total control, no improvisation, and if you touch something without permission, you lose the hand.

She inspects walls. She reviews mortar compositions. She checks resonance readings on instruments she designed herself. And she has a proposal before the Council: SYNTHETIC RESONANCE GENERATORS. Machines that replicate the guardian purr frequency. Mechanical purrs, produced on schedule, without the inconvenience of depending on living animals.

Her argument is practical: "What happens when the next border incursion comes? What happens when they cage OUR cats the way they caged Skelya's? We lose our guardians, we lose our walls, we lose everything. The generators ensure the city survives regardless of what happens to the cats."

The council debates. Sirko sits in the Middle.

Marko watches from the public gallery, deeply uneasy but unable to articulate why. Panas, next to him, can: "She's building a city that doesn't need us." He means keepers. He means cats. He means the relationship between the two.

What nobody on the council knows: Ruda was a keeper. Twenty years ago, her guardian, a silver tabby named MYKYTA, was captured during the last border incursion. Ruda fought to free him. She heard him scream through the muzzle. She never saw him again.

She stopped being a keeper that day. She decided: the problem wasn't that Mykyta wasn't strong enough. The problem was that she'd loved something that could be taken from her.

She became the walls instead.

---

## THE MIDNIGHT HARNESS

Two weeks in. Kalyna can't sleep (she forgot to re-tie the hammock after washing it). She goes outside at 2 AM to fix Bila's harness — the Murchyk-style buckles are different from Skelya's, and her hands are cracked from the cold.

Marko appears. Sits ten feet away. Pulls out his own leather, his own tools. Starts stitching a completely new harness. Not looking at her. Not offering. Just working nearby.

She watches him. He works. Bila watches both of them, tail curling slowly — the first slow-curl she's done since arriving.

Neither speaks for forty minutes.

Kalyna falls asleep against Bila's side. In the morning, the new harness is next to her. Murchyk buckles, Skelya stitching. A hybrid. Like he studied how she made hers and adapted.

She finds him at the grooming station. Holds it up.

"Did you —"

"Buckle faces outward."

"...Thank you."

"It's for the cat. Badly fitted harnesses cause rash."

---

## THE GENERATORS

Ruda wins the vote. The synthetic generators are installed in the Eastern Quarter as a pilot. They hum to life at 10 PM — a precise, engineered frequency, exactly matching the guardian purr on every measurable metric.

And the Eastern Quarter cats stop purring.

Not because they're forced to. Because the generators ALREADY produce the frequency. The cats don't purr redundantly. They sense the resonance in the stone is maintained and they... relax. Go quiet. Sleep.

For three days, everything seems fine. The walls hold. The stone doesn't crack.

Then: rooftop gardens stop growing. The herbs in the Eastern Quarter wilt. Bread doesn't rise properly. Children start having nightmares again — the kind the purr usually prevents. A baker complains. A mother complains. The Council says: "The resonance readings are identical. It must be psychological."

But Marko knows it isn't. He can feel it in his floor. The vibration is correct in frequency but wrong in... something. It's like the difference between a person speaking and a recording of a person speaking. Technically identical. Emotionally nothing.

Kalyna feels it too. She knows this silence. This is what Skelya sounded like the week before it fell. The walls standing but the life draining out.

She starts packing.

Marko finds her stuffing clothes into a bag.

"Where are you going?"

"Same place as always. Away."

He wants to say STAY. Instead:

"The eastern ramps are iced over. Bila's paw pads aren't conditioned for Murchyk cold yet. If she slips at her weight, she could fracture a joint. You should wait until morning."

She stares at him.

"Is that what you came to say? The ramps are icy?"

Long pause.

"...The ramps are icy."

---

## THE MEDALLION

Kalyna doesn't leave. Not because of the ramps. Because of what Bila does that night.

Bila walks to the Eastern Wall and presses her forehead against the stone. And PURRS. Alone. Against the silence of the generators. One cat, purring into a wall that has forgotten what real purring sounds like.

The stone around Bila's head begins to glow. Faintly. A warmth in the mortar that the generators can't produce.

Kalyna watches. Then she reaches into the lining of her coat — the one thing she saved from Skelya — and pulls out a RESONANCE KEY. A medallion of Skelya stone, the size of a fist, humming with a frequency that makes Bila's ears twitch.

She'd been carrying her dead city's heartbeat against her chest since the day it fell.

She shows Marko. He holds it. Feels it vibrate. And for the first time, his careful, practical, by-the-rules face changes. He looks scared. And excited. And completely out of his depth.

"This needs to go to the Council."

"They'll study it and file it and form a committee and nothing will happen."

"That's how things work here. We have procedures."

"That's how things worked in Skelya too. Right up until the first cage rolled in. Procedures. Committees. 'We're monitoring the situation.' You know what procedures are? They're what people do instead of fighting."

Silence.

"...Fine. We do it at night."

"You'd break the rules?"

He doesn't look at her. "Buckle faces outward."

---

## THE FIRST BRIDGE

They embed the medallion in the First Bridge at midnight. Marko mixes the mortar — Murchyk limestone with ground Skelya stone from the medallion's casing. Kalyna sets the key into the bridge's central support column. Bila stands guard, ears rotating.

They wait.

Hours pass. Nothing happens. The stone doesn't glow. The bridge doesn't hum. Bila sits by the column, silent.

Kalyna sits on the cold stone:

"I dragged you into this. I drag everyone into my disasters. Skelya. My family. And now you. Everyone who stays near me loses something."

Marko wants to say: *You didn't drag me. I chose this. I chose you.* Instead:

"The mortar needs time to cure. Limestone and Skelya stone have different thermal properties. Expansion takes longer in cold weather. We'll know by morning."

She looks at him. Tears.

"I'm telling you I ruin everything I touch and you're talking about MORTAR?"

His voice breaks.

"...The mortar needs time."

---

## RUDA DISCOVERS

Dawn. The medallion hasn't activated. But Ruda's instruments detect the anomaly — a foreign resonance in the First Bridge. She traces it. Finds the embedded stone. And she's FURIOUS.

Not just at the rule-breaking. At what it represents: dependency. Hope placed in a living thing instead of an engineered solution.

She summons Marko and Kalyna to the Council chamber. Bila follows. And Bila sits on the evidence table. Right on top of the documents Ruda needs for her presentation. Nobody can move her.

Ruda, trying to pull a document from under Bila's haunches: "If... the council... could reference... page... the cat is on page three."

The room almost laughs. Ruda doesn't.

Then, privately, after the hearing:

"You embedded a foreign resonance into our oldest bridge. Without approval. Without testing. Do you understand what you've risked?"

"The bridge held."

"THIS TIME." She turns to Kalyna. "And you. I know what you're carrying. I know what it feels like to hold onto something from a city that's gone. But embedding it here doesn't bring Skelya back. It puts Murchyk at risk."

Kalyna: "It's not about bringing Skelya back—"

Ruda: "It's ALWAYS about bringing it back. You think I don't know? I lost my cat twenty years ago. They muzzled him and I heard him scream and I never saw him again. And I spent five years trying to find him. Five years carrying his collar against my chest, just like you carry that stone. And do you know what those five years gave me? NOTHING. Except the knowledge that loving something you can't protect is the cruelest thing you can do to yourself."

She turns to Marko.

"I loved my cat MORE than you love yours. More. I would have died for Mykyta. And when they took him, I wanted to. So don't tell me the cats are enough. They weren't enough for me."

Silence.

Marko, quietly: "They weren't enough alone. The walls weren't enough alone either."

Ruda: "The walls are still standing."

Marko: "Standing and alive aren't the same thing."

---

## THE EMPIRE ARRIVES

Smoke on the eastern horizon. Cage-wagons. The empire has reached Murchyk's border.

The city mobilizes — but differently than Skelya. Ruda's walls are strong. The generators hum at full power. Archers take positions. The cats pace, agitated. They sense what's coming but the generators are producing the purr frequency, so the cats don't purr themselves. They're confused. The engineered frequency says *everything is fine.* Their instincts say *everything is not fine.*

The first attack hits the Eastern Wall. Empire soldiers with RESONANCE DAMPENERS — iron devices that emit a counter-frequency, designed to cancel the purr and crack the stone. Against a natural purr, it would be a battle of living will versus machine. Against Ruda's generators, it's machine versus machine.

And machine versus machine, the dampeners win.

The generators sputter. The frequency destabilizes. The Eastern Wall begins to crack — not slowly, the way natural purr-decay works, but SUDDENLY, in jagged fractures, because synthetic resonance has no adaptive response. It doesn't fight back. It just fails.

Ruda, at the wall, watching her instruments go red: "No. No. Increase the output —"

"The oscillators are at maximum. The dampeners are calibrated to our exact frequency."

Of course they are. Because the empire didn't need to guess. Synthetic frequencies are PRECISE. Predictable. Copyable. A living purr varies moment to moment, cat to cat, impossible to counter-match. The generators' greatest strength — their precision — is their fatal weakness.

The Eastern Wall begins to fall.

---

## THE CHOICE

Ruda stands at the generator controls. Two options.

She can reroute power from other districts to boost the Eastern generators. It might hold the wall — but it will drain the generators protecting the rest of the city. Everything on one mechanical bet.

Or she can shut the generators down entirely and let the guardians PURR. Real purring. Living frequency. Unpredictable, uncopyable, impossible to dampen — because every cat produces a slightly different harmonic at every moment, and the dampeners can't match what they can't predict.

But that means DEPENDING on the cats. Trusting that they'll purr. Trusting that living things will do what she needs them to do, when she needs them to do it. The one thing she swore she'd never do again.

Marko, beside her: "Let them purr."

Ruda: "If they don't —"

"They will."

"You don't KNOW that. You can't know that. Mykyta didn't —" She stops herself.

Kalyna, from behind: "Bila will."

Ruda looks at Kalyna. At the white cat pressed against her leg. At the bond between them — the thing Ruda lost, the thing she's spent twenty years building walls against.

She shuts the generators down.

Silence. Total silence. The Eastern Wall creaks. Stone dust falls.

And Bila purrs.

One cat, pressing her forehead against the First Bridge, where the Skelya medallion is embedded. The medallion responds — not to the frequency, but to the LIFE in the frequency. It amplifies. The bridge hums. The vibration travels through the stone, through the foundations, toward the Eastern Wall.

The guardians feel it. One by one. Two by two. Then all at once.

Every cat in Murchyk purrs.

Not in unison — that's what the generators did. In HARMONY. Each cat producing its own frequency, its own rhythm, its own living vibration. The harmonics interweave. The dampeners spin wildly trying to counter-match and CAN'T — the frequency shifts every second, alive and unpredictable, a choir that no machine can silence because no two voices sing the same note twice.

The Eastern Wall stops cracking. The stone knits. Not back to what it was — better. Skelya's frequency and Murchyk's frequency woven together in the mortar, two dead and living cities singing through the same stone.

The empire's dampeners overheat. Spark. Go silent.

The wall holds.

---

## THEY STOP DEFLECTING

After. The city holds. Dawn light on the First Bridge, still humming with the Skelya resonance. Marko and Kalyna sit on the bridge, exhausted, covered in limestone dust. Bila sleeps between them, purring softly.

"I wasn't fixing the harness."

"I know."

"I wasn't checking the ramps. I wasn't waiting for mortar."

"I know."

"I don't know how to say what I'm trying to say. Every time I open my mouth, buckles come out. And ramps. And mortar. I've spent my whole life talking to cats because cats don't need me to use the right words. They just need me to show up. And I—"

"You showed up."

Beat.

"The mortar cured."

"Oh my god, stop talking about mortar."

Bila, between them, purrs louder.

---

## RUDA

After the battle. Ruda inspects damage to the Eastern Wall. The cracks have sealed, fused with something new — a hybrid resonance, Murchyk and Skelya woven together. She runs her hand along the stone. Feels the vibration. The real one. The one she hasn't felt in twenty years.

She stands there for a long time.

A small stray kitten — not a guardian, just a street kitten, orange, with one bent ear — rubs against her ankle. She looks down. Stiffens. Doesn't pick it up.

But she doesn't kick it away either.

She keeps walking. The kitten follows.

She doesn't look back.

But she slows down. Just slightly. So it can keep up.

---

## RESOLUTION

The Eastern Quarter hums again. Not with generators — with cats. Rooftop gardens grow back. Bread rises. Children sleep without nightmares.

At the fish market, Olek adjusts mackerel prices downward by one coin in celebration. The city's economy lurches. Three keepers lose a bet. A guardian knocks a flower pot off a ledge — it's not chained. It shatters. The shopkeeper sighs: "I JUST unchained those."

At the grooming station, Marko fits Bila with a new harness. Murchyk buckles, Skelya stitching. Bila holds still for him — the first time she's allowed a keeper other than Kalyna to touch her.

Kalyna watches from across the square. The same position Marko watched from the day she arrived. The same distance. But this time, she walks over.

"Buckle faces outward?"

"Buckle faces outward."

Night falls. The purr-curfew begins. The city vibrates. On the First Bridge, the Skelya medallion glows faintly in the mortar — a small warm light, like a heartbeat from a city that died but whose voice still sings through the stone of the city that took its people in.

Bila lies on the bridge, forehead pressed to the stone, purring. Her frequency and the medallion's frequency harmonize — two notes that shouldn't fit together, from two places that are nothing alike, making a sound that neither could make alone.

Marko and Kalyna sit in their hammocks, in their separate rooms, in their separate buildings, on opposite sides of the square. Both listening to the purr. Both awake.

Both smiling.

**THE END**
