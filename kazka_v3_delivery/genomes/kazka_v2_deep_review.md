## What Works (The Good Stuff)

### 1. The Anna Scene is a Masterpiece of Character Edge
Beat 09 is the emotional center of gravity. Not the finale — this. A displaced girl tells a comfortable German child her problems aren't real. It's cruel. It's understandable. It's inexcusable. And Olenka KNOWS it's wrong but can't bring herself to apologize because "knocking means admitting that her pain and Anna's pain can coexist, and that means the world is more complicated than 'I have it worse.'"

The 8-second static camera with no score while Anna cries through the bathroom wall — that's not animation. That's cinema.

### 2. The Thematic Paradox Actually Works
Halyna says "remember everything." The therapist says "release." The film argues both sides through REAL characters, not strawmen. Dr. Berger is not wrong — she's just limited. Halyna is not right — she put an impossible burden on a child. When Berger says "Yes" to Olenka's realization that there's no answer, it's the most honest moment in the document. And it's three seconds of silence.

### 3. Fairy Tales as Panic Attacks
This is the concept that makes this genome work. Every other "fairy tales come to life" story treats them as allies or adventures. Here they're involuntary, destructive, and they COST something. The cost mechanism (each manifestation eats a real memory) creates genuine escalating stakes. By the midpoint, Olenka can't remember if Halyna had brown or green eyes. By All Is Lost, she can't see Halyna's face at all.

### 4. Lys is Not Nick Wilde
v1's Lys was a reskinned Nick Wilde — cynical exterior, heart of gold. v2's Lys is pre-moral. He doesn't understand "should." He manipulates for amusement. His relationship with Olenka ends not as friendship but as "something unnameable." His shadow is "always too large, shaped like something with more legs than he has." That's an unsettling detail that earns the psychological horror genre.

## What Fails (The Bad Stuff)

### 1. Cultural Authenticity Has a Procedural Gap (0.82)
The emotional truth of displacement is beautifully rendered. The LOGISTICS are hand-waved. A Ukrainian refugee moving through France → Switzerland → Germany → UK requires legal explanations that aren't here. Someone who's been through the system would notice. One paragraph of Dublin III / TPD / case transfer language would fix this.

### 2. Elif is a Plot Function, Not a Character
"A Turkish-German girl who also knows what it's like to be from two places at once" — that's a description, not a person. She helps organize the community center. She deserves one scene where she's a full human being with her own displacement pain and her own pushback against Olenka.

### 3. The Mother is a Ghost
Olenka's mother makes every relocation decision. She's caught between Halyna's "remember" and Berger's "let go." She's living the paradox as a parent. And she has no scene. No confrontation. No moment of her own. This is a major untapped emotional resource.

### 4. "Somewhere in the United States" is Lazy
The final image places Olenka in an unnamed American city. "It doesn't matter which one." In a genome that specifies "IKEA KALLAX shelves still in the box" and "someone else's curtains — yellow, with daisies," this is the one moment of anti-specificity. Keep her in Europe. The story earns that geography.

## Fix Suggestions (Specific, Not Vague)

1. **Add legal context** — one line: "Their temporary protection application was transferred under Dublin III after the Calais incident" — proves the writer knows the system.
2. **Make Lys's arrival on the train dangerous** — he should be triggered by a specific emotion, the other passengers should react (a child points, a dog whines), his first words should be threatening.
3. **Change Final Image to Berlin** — she's in Neukölln. Where she hurt Anna. Where she came back to apologize. The geography completes.
4. **Give Elif a real scene** — she tells Olenka about her own grandmother's stories from Turkey. She's not just a helper. She's a mirror.
5. **Give the Mother a confrontation** — between beats 09 and 10. "I'm doing everything I can and you're making it impossible." The mother is living the paradox too.
6. **Break the manifestation pattern** — the third manifestation (Rusalka) should cost a memory of OLENKA, not Halyna. She forgets her own middle name. Escalation is non-linear.
