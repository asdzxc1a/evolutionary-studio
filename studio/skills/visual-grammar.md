# Visual Grammar for Animation

## Shot Sizes

| Shot | Abbreviation | Use Case |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Extreme Wide Shot** | EWS | Establish location, scale, isolation |
| **Wide Shot** | WS | Full body, action, relationship positioning |
| **Medium Shot** | MS | Waist up, dialogue, emotion |
| **Close-Up** | CU | Face, intense emotion, reaction |
| **Extreme Close-Up** | ECU | Eyes, hands, detail — intimacy or tension |
| **Over-the-Shoulder** | OTS | Conversation, power dynamic |
| **Two-Shot** | 2S | Relationship framing, partnership |
| **Point of View** | POV | Immersion, subjectivity |
| **Aerial** | AERIAL | Scale, power, overview |

### Animation-Specific Notes
- **Eyes are everything in animation.** A CU of eyes communicates more than a WS of action.
- **Use wide shots for physical comedy.** Animation exaggerates movement — wide shots let the body do the work.
- **Close-ups for emotional beats.** The audience needs to see the subtle facial animation that took 40 hours to craft.

## Camera Movement

| Movement | Emotional Effect |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Static** | Stability, observation, documentary feel |
| **Push-in** | Growing intimacy, realization, threat |
| **Pull-out** | Reveal, isolation, scale realization |
| **Pan** | Searching, comparing, landscape |
| **Tilt** | Awe, power, vertigo |
| **Dolly** | Smooth following, fluid emotion |
| **Handheld** | Documentary, chaos, realism (use sparingly in animation) |
| **Crane** | Epic, transition, freedom |

### The Push-In Rule
The push-in is the most powerful tool in animation. Use it for:
- Character realization ("I was wrong about everything")
- Threat escalation (antagonist reveals true power)
- Intimacy peak (two characters share a truth)

**Never push-in without emotional justification.** A push-in on exposition is visual noise.

## Lighting Schemes

| Scheme | Mood | Use Case |
|--------|------|----------|
| **High-key** | Optimistic, safe, daytime | Comedy, setup, hometown |
| **Low-key** | Dangerous, mysterious, tense | Thriller, Act 2B, antagonist |
| **Chiaroscuro** | Dramatic, noir, moral ambiguity | Midpoint turn, revelation |
| **Color wash** | Emotional tone, stylized | Memory, dream, emotional peak |
| **Silhouette** | Anonymous, powerful, iconic | Hero moment, transformation |
| **Underlight** | Horror, unnatural, fear | Monsters, nightmares, lies revealed |

### Animation Lighting Principles
- **Light defines form.** In CG animation, lighting is sculpting.
- **Color temperature = emotional temperature.** Warm = safety/love. Cool = danger/isolation.
- **Contrast draws attention.** The eye goes to the brightest area first. Place your emotional focus there.

## Composition Rules

### Rule of Thirds
Place focal points at intersection lines. Never center the horizon.

### Leading Lines
Use architecture, roads, shadows to direct the eye to the focal point.

### Framing Within Frames
Doorways, windows, arches create visual depth and containment.

### Negative Space
Isolation = large negative space. Claustrophobia = tight framing with clutter.

### Scale Contrast
Small character + large environment = vulnerability. Large character + small environment = power.

## Scene Pacing Through Editing

### The 3-Second Rule
In animation, the audience needs 3 seconds to register a new shot. Cut faster only for:
- Action sequences (1-2 seconds)
- Montages (rapid-fire)
- Chaos/panic (disorienting)

### The Hold
Hold on a static frame for:
- Reaction shots (let the audience feel with the character)
- Revelations (give the moment weight)
- Beauty shots (the world itself is the point)

**Animation holds are longer than live-action.** The audience needs time to absorb the crafted image.

### Transition Types
| Transition | Use |
|------------|-----|
| **Cut** | Standard, invisible, time passes |
| **Dissolve** | Time passes, memory, softening |
| **Wipe** | Geography change, comedy, stylized |
| **Match cut** | Thematic connection, visual rhyme |
| **Smash cut** | Juxtaposition, shock, comedy |
