# Story Structure Craft — Animation Feature

> **Purpose:** Teach the system's agents how to structure animated feature films at a professional level. Structure serves character. Character serves theme. Never the reverse.

---

## Save the Cat Beats (Animation-Specific)

### Opening Image
- Meta-sequels, cold opens, and visual gags are the norm in modern animation.
- Must establish tone in **60 seconds or less**.
- The opening image is a promise to the audience: *"This is the kind of movie you're watching."*
- **Animation twist:** The opening often features a character doing something ordinary in an extraordinary way (or vice versa). A rabbit farming carrots on a massive industrial scale. A fox conning a popsicle out of a jumbo pop.
- **Meta-sequel bonus:** Reference the first film's opening image, then subvert it.

### Theme Stated
- Usually delivered by a **well-meaning but wrong authority figure**.
- The audience should recognize the irony immediately; the protagonist should not.
- The theme is stated as advice the protagonist will spend the entire film learning is incomplete or wrong.
- **Example:** "Trust only your own kind" — stated sincerely by a parent or boss. The film will prove this false.

### Setup
- **Parallel routines for co-protagonists.** Show don't tell their differences.
- Cross-cut between Character A's morning and Character B's morning.
- Each routine should reveal: their flaw, their desire, their coping mechanism, their environment.
- The two routines should mirror each other thematically even as they contrast visually.
- **Rule:** If the audience can't describe each protagonist's flaw in one sentence by minute 8, the setup has failed.

### Catalyst
- The inciting incident must be **VISUAL and SPECIFIC**.
- **Never:** "A case goes missing."
- **Always:** "A crate splits open revealing reptile skin. A toxic flower blooms in the rain. A mushroom cloud rises over the savanna."
- The catalyst must be something the protagonist physically sees or experiences. It cannot be reported secondhand.
- In animation, the catalyst often involves a visual transformation or the arrival of something that doesn't belong.

### Debate
- The debate is about **personal stakes, not world-ending stakes**.
- **Wrong:** "Should we take this case?" (procedural)
- **Right:** "Are we good enough?" (emotional)
- The debate must reveal the protagonist's core fear. What are they afraid will happen if they say yes?
- In co-protagonist films, each character debates separately — and for different reasons.

### B-Story Launch
- The B-story must **mirror the A-story thematically**.
- If A is "solve the mystery," B is "fix the relationship."
- If A is "save the city," B is "learn to trust."
- The B-story characters must be introduced in Act 1 and pay off in Act 3.
- The B-story launch beat (around 30%) should feel like a detour — but it's actually the true story.

### Fun & Games
- **Comedy set pieces that explore the premise.**
- Each gag must reveal character. A joke that any character could tell is a wasted joke.
- The "promise of the premise" lives here. If your premise is "a rabbit cop and a fox con-man solve crimes," this section must deliver on that promise.
- **Rule of three:** Three set pieces, escalating in scale and consequence.
- Visual gags should outnumber verbal gags 2:1.

### Midpoint
- **False victory OR false defeat.**
- The partnership must fracture here — or the fracture must become visible.
- In a **false victory:** The protagonists achieve their goal but realize it was the wrong goal, or it came at a cost they didn't anticipate.
- In a **false defeat:** The protagonists lose everything — but the loss reveals what truly matters.
- The midpoint is the **fulcrum of the entire film**. Everything before leads to it; everything after is the consequence.
- **Animation note:** The midpoint often involves a public humiliation or a public success that privately destroys.

### Bad Guys Close In
- The conspiracy deepens. The theme gets darker.
- Internal opposition (doubt, guilt) must match external opposition (antagonist, system).
- The world closes in on the protagonist from all sides.
- In co-protagonist films, this is where the characters turn on each other.
- **Rule:** The antagonist's plan must become clear to the audience before it becomes clear to the protagonists.

### All Is Lost
- The **"whiff of death"** — but in animation, it's almost always **emotional death**, not physical death.
- The death of the partnership. The death of a dream. The death of identity.
- Something must be lost that cannot be easily recovered. A trust. A reputation. A relationship.
- The "whiff of death" should echo a death from the protagonist's backstory.

### Dark Night of the Soul
- **Despair.** The protagonist admits failure — to themselves or to someone else.
- This is the quietest scene in the film. No action. No comedy. Just truth.
- The protagonist must say the thing they've been afraid to say.
- **Animation note:** Even in despair, keep the character's voice consistent. A trickster in despair makes self-deprecating jokes that don't land.

### Break into Three
- The **B-story solution enables the A-story climax.**
- The lesson learned in the relationship subplot becomes the weapon for the external plot.
- This is why the B-story matters. If you can solve the A-story without the B-story, the B-story is broken.
- The break into three should feel earned — the protagonist has changed, and that change is visible.

### Finale
- The **5-point finale**:
  1. **Gather team** — Assemble allies, resources, and courage.
  2. **Execute plan** — The plan starts working.
  3. **High tower surprise** — The plan fails. A twist reveals the true stakes.
  4. **Dig deep down** — The protagonist uses everything they've learned. The theme is tested.
  5. **New world** — The world is transformed. The protagonist has changed it.
- Each point should escalate. The high tower surprise should feel inevitable in retrospect but shocking in the moment.

### Final Image
- **Mirror of the opening image, but transformed.**
- The same visual idea, seen through the lens of everything that happened.
- **Example:** The carrot pen that recorded confessions now records love.
- **Example:** The door the protagonist couldn't open in Act 1 is now open — and they're walking through it with someone else.
- The final image is proof of transformation. Show, don't tell.

## B-Story Mechanics

### Structural Requirements
- The B-story **must have its own 3-act structure**:
  - Act 1: Characters meet, conflict is established.
  - Act 2: Bonding moment, then fracture.
  - Act 3: Reconciliation enables A-story climax.
- The B-story climax **must happen BEFORE the A-story climax**.
- The A-story climax is powered by the emotional resolution of the B-story.

### Character Requirements
- B-story characters **must appear in Act 1** and **pay off in Act 3**.
- A character introduced in Act 2 for B-story purposes feels like a plot device.
- The B-story relationship must be tested in the same way the A-story is tested.

### Thematic Requirements
- The B-story must ask the same thematic question as the A-story, but in a different key.
- If A asks "Can predators and prey coexist?" then B asks "Can Judy and Nick trust each other?"
- The B-story answer must be "yes" before the A-story answer can be "yes."

## C-Story Mechanics (Systemic / Historical)

### Definition
- The C-story is the **"world" story** — history, politics, systems, institutions.
- It's the water the characters swim in. They don't always see it, but the audience must.

### Requirements
- The C-story revelation **must recontextualize the A-story**.
- The personal conflict becomes a systemic conflict. The crime reveals a conspiracy.
- The C-story must be **shown visually, not explained in dialogue**.
- **Bad:** A character gives a five-minute history lecture.
- **Good:** A visual montage of newspaper headlines, faded photographs, and crumbling monuments that tell the history without words.

### Integration
- C-story clues must be planted in Act 1 (background details the audience barely notices).
- C-story revelation happens in Act 2B or early Act 3.
- C-story resolution happens in the finale (the system changes, or the character rejects the system).

## Animation-Specific Structure Notes

### Rhythm Requirements
- **Action sequence every 8-12 minutes.** Animation audiences need kinetic energy.
- **Emotional beat every 12-15 minutes.** The heart must beat between explosions.
- **Visual gag every 2-3 minutes.** Even in drama, the eye needs candy.

### Pacing Rules
- **Third act is 15-20% faster than first act.** Momentum builds; it never slows.
- If Act 1 is 25 minutes, Act 3 should feel like 20 minutes of screen time even if it's the same length.
- Achieve this through shorter scenes, cross-cutting, and raised stakes.

### Cold Open Rules
- **Cold open can be 2-5 minutes.**
- The cold open is a mini-movie with its own setup-catalyst-payoff.
- It can be from the past (backstory), the future (flash-forward), or a parallel story (side character).
- The cold open must thematically echo the main story.

### End Credits
- **End credits sequence is part of the storytelling.**
- It resolves lingering questions, shows the new world in action, or delivers one final emotional beat.
- In animation, end credits often feature a visual gag or a callback that rewards attentive viewers.

### Scene Metrics
- **45-65 scenes for a 90-minute film.** Average scene: 90 seconds.
- **Dialogue ratio:** Action beats should exceed dialogue lines. Target **1.5:1 action-to-dialogue**.
- Animation is visual. If a scene can be told without words, cut the words.

## Common Structure Failures

1. **Act 1 is too long.** If your catalyst hasn't happened by minute 12, cut.
2. **Midpoint doesn't fracture the partnership.** In co-protagonist films, the midpoint must test the relationship.
3. **B-story is detachable.** If you can remove the B-story and the A-story still works, the B-story is broken.
4. **All Is Lost is too easy.** The protagonist must lose something they cannot easily recover.
5. **Finale has no high tower surprise.** A climax without a twist is a resolution without a test.
6. **Theme is stated but never tested.** The theme must be challenged in every act.
