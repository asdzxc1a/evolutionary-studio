# Critic Scoring Criteria

## Structure Critic (Weight: 0.25)

### What We Score
Does the story have a coherent three-act structure with proper beat placement?

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **9-10** | All beats present and correctly placed. Midpoint is a genuine turning point. Climax tests transformed hero. Act breaks are clean. |
| **7-8** | Most beats present. Midpoint exists but turn is weak. Climax is active. One act may be underdeveloped. |
| **5-6** | Basic structure present but missing key beats (e.g., no clear All Is Lost). Midpoint is a plot point, not a turn. |
| **3-4** | Structure is vague. Acts blur together. No clear midpoint or climax. |
| **0-2** | No recognizable structure. Random scenes. |

### Red Flags
- Midpoint is not at 50% (or doesn't exist)
- No "All Is Lost" moment in Act 2B
- Climax is passive (things happen TO hero, not BECAUSE of hero)
- Act 1 is longer than Act 3
- No B-story or relationship subplot

### Green Flags
- Midpoint raises stakes AND changes direction
- "All Is Lost" feels genuinely hopeless
- Climax forces hero to use new skill/wisdom from Act 2
- Opening Image and Final Image are thematic opposites

## Emotion Critic (Weight: 0.25)

### What We Score
Does the emotional arc follow a satisfying curve? Does the audience feel what they should feel at each beat?

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **9-10** | Strong emotional range (-0.8 to +0.9). Recovery time respected. Climax resolves cathartically. Every scene has clear emotional valence. |
| **7-8** | Good range with one weak section. Recovery mostly respected. Climax is positive but not transcendent. |
| **5-6** | Moderate range. Some scenes lack emotional clarity. Recovery times too short. Climax is okay. |
| **3-4** | Flat emotional arc. All scenes feel similar. No genuine lows or highs. |
| **0-2** | Incoherent emotions. Audience can't tell what to feel. |

### Red Flags
- No scene below -0.5 valence (no genuine darkness)
- No scene above +0.5 valence (no genuine joy)
- Negative beat immediately followed by another negative beat (no recovery)
- Climax resolves below +0.5 (unsatisfying for animated comedy-drama)
- "All Is Lost" feels like a minor inconvenience

### Green Flags
- Opening: slightly positive (curiosity, hope)
- Midpoint: false victory (+0.6) OR false defeat (-0.4)
- All Is Lost: genuine despair (-0.8)
- Break into Three: first glimmer of hope (+0.2)
- Climax: cathartic triumph (+0.8 to +0.9)

### The 90-Second Rule
After any beat below -0.6, the audience needs 90 seconds of recovery before the next major stressor. This can be:
- Comedy relief
- Beautiful imagery
- Character bonding
- Quiet moment of reflection

## Pacing Critic (Weight: 0.20)

### What We Score
Does the story move at the right speed? Are scenes the right length? Is there visual variety?

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **9-10** | Hook under 2 min. Climax 5+ min. Action beats exceed dialogue. Scene lengths vary (30s to 3min). No drag. |
| **7-8** | Hook slightly long or climax slightly short. Good action/dialogue ratio. Minor pacing inconsistencies. |
| **5-6** | Hook too long OR climax too short. Dialogue-heavy sections. Some scenes feel padded. |
| **3-4** | Pacing is monotonous. All scenes same length. Too much talking, not enough showing. |
| **0-2** | Incoherent pacing. Hook is 10 minutes. Climax is 30 seconds. |

### Red Flags
- Hook over 3 minutes (audience loses patience)
- Climax under 2 minutes (no time to build spectacle)
- Dialogue lines exceed action beats 2:1 (too talky for animation)
- All scenes are 90 seconds (monotonous)
- Act 2A and Act 2B same length (should be 2A shorter, 2B longer)

### Green Flags
- Hook: 60-120 seconds, high energy
- Act 1: 20-25 minutes (fast setup)
- Act 2A: 15-20 minutes (fun & games)
- Act 2B: 20-25 minutes (pressure mounts)
- Act 3: 15-20 minutes (climax + resolution)
- Action-to-dialogue ratio: 1.5:1

## Theme Critic (Weight: 0.30)

### What We Score
Is the theme clear, consistent, and explored with nuance? Is the social metaphor specific and two-sided?

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **9-10** | Theme is present in every act but never stated directly. Social metaphor is specific and challenges both sides. Show-don't-tell ratio excellent. |
| **7-8** | Theme is clear. Social metaphor is specific. One or two on-the-nose moments. |
| **5-6** | Theme is present but inconsistent across acts. Social metaphor is vague. Characters state theme aloud. |
| **3-4** | Theme is unclear or absent. No social metaphor. Preachy dialogue. |
| **0-2** | No theme. Random events with no meaning. |

### Red Flags
- Character says the theme directly ("The real lesson is...")
- Theme only appears in Act 3 (tacked on)
- Social metaphor is one-sided (only one group is wrong)
- Theme words appear in >15% of dialogue lines
- No visual representation of theme (only verbal)

### Green Flags
- Theme is shown through ACTION, not dialogue
- Social metaphor is two-sided (Zootopia: BOTH predators AND prey have prejudice)
- Theme evolves: Act 1 establishes it simply, Act 3 reveals its complexity
- Visual motifs reinforce theme (Zootopia: size = power, species = class)
- Antagonist believes a version of the theme (they're not wrong, they're incomplete)

### The Two-Sided Test
A great theme has nuance. Ask:
- Does the protagonist's initial belief have SOME truth? (Judy: "Anyone can be anything" — true, but incomplete)
- Does the antagonist's belief have SOME truth? (Bellwether: "Predators are dangerous" — statistically true, but used to justify oppression)
- Does the resolution find a THIRD way? (Zootopia: Trust requires acknowledging differences, not pretending they don't exist)

If the theme has only one right answer, it's propaganda, not art.
