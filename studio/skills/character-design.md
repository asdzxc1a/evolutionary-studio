# Character Design Craft — Animation Feature

> **Purpose:** Teach the system how to design characters with the depth, specificity, and consistency seen in professional animated features (Zootopia 2 as primary reference).

---

## The Lie-Truth Arc

Every memorable character is built on a **Lie they believe** and a **Truth they must learn**.

### Positive Change Arc (Most Common)
**Structure:** Wounded → Acts on Lie → Midpoint glimpse → Tested → Chooses Truth → Healed

**Examples:**
- Judy Hopps: "Anyone can be anything" (Lie: hard work erases prejudice) → Learns that trust requires vulnerability
- Nick Wilde: "Never let them see they get to you" (Lie: cynicism protects) → Learns that vulnerability is strength
- Gary (Zootopia 2): "I don't belong anywhere" (Lie: belonging must be earned through sacrifice) → Learns that belonging comes from being seen

### Flat Arc (Steadfast Hero)
**Structure:** Knows Truth → Acts on Truth → Tested → Truth wins → World changes

**Examples:**
- Nibbles (Zootopia 2): Knows loyalty matters more than size → Never wavers → Inspires others to be brave

### Negative Change Arc (Tragedy / Fall)
**Structure:** Has chance → Chooses Lie → Commits → Doubles down → Cost revealed → Tragic end

**Examples:**
- Pawbert (Zootopia 2): Could choose honesty → Chooses theft for love → Destroys himself and those he loves

## Archetype Definitions (Zootopia 2 Specific)

### The Cynical Trickster (Nick Wilde)

**Surface:** Sarcastic, lazy, morally flexible, always has an angle.

**Truth:** Deeply loyal, traumatized by childhood betrayal, uses humor as armor to prevent anyone from getting close enough to hurt him again.

**Voice Dimensions:**
| Dimension | Score | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Sarcasm | 90 | Irony is default mode. Deflects every genuine moment. |
| Cynicism | 85 | Expects the worst from systems and people. Often right. |
| Warmth | 40 (hidden) | Shows care through actions, never words. Rises to 70 by finale. |
| Formality | 20 | Contractions, slang, nicknames. "Carrots." "Slick." |
| Verbosity | 70 | Can talk his way out of anything. Uses words as weapons and shields. |
| Subtext | 95 | Almost never says what he means. The real message is in what he doesn't say. |

**Forbidden Phrases:**
- Direct emotional confession without deflection
- "I love you" without a joke immediately before or after
- Admitting fear without framing it as someone else's problem
- Trusting authority figures openly

**Signature Lines Pattern:**
1. Self-deprecating aside
2. Pop culture reference or playful insult
3. Accidental truth (the real feeling slips through)

**Example:**
> NICK: I'm not saying I'm a hero. I'm just saying if this goes wrong, it's definitely Judy's fault. ...But also, I wouldn't let her do it alone. Obviously. ...Obviously.

**Physical Comedy:**
- Slouches to appear smaller and less threatening
- Wears sunglasses to hide his eyes (the windows to his actual feelings)
- Uses props as emotional crutches (the pen, his tie, his glasses)
- Touch-averse except with specific people; a casual arm around Judy is a huge character moment

### The Earnest Believer (Judy Hopps)

**Surface:** Optimistic, hardworking, rule-follower, wants to make the world better.

**Truth:** Insecure, terrified of failure, overcompensates for being small/underestimated by working twice as hard. Her optimism is sometimes a performance she puts on for others — and for herself.

**Voice Dimensions:**
| Dimension | Score | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Sarcasm | 10 | Almost never. When she does, it's awkward and unconvincing. |
| Cynicism | 15 | Wants to believe in systems. Disappointment hurts because she trusted. |
| Warmth | 85 | Genuinely cares. Sometimes cares too much, too openly. |
| Formality | 60 | Uses full names, titles, proper grammar. "Officer Hopps reporting for duty." |
| Verbosity | 80 | Over-explains. Lists reasons on her fingers. Justifies everything. |
| Subtext | 30 | Says what she means. Sometimes too directly. Emotional transparency is her default. |

**Forbidden Phrases:**
- Giving up without a fight
- Admitting weakness without immediately justifying how she'll overcome it
- Breaking rules without extensive internal debate
- Being cruel (even when deserved)

**Signature Lines Pattern:**
1. Lists reasons (on fingers, if possible)
2. Justifies with rules, statistics, or logic
3. Motivational speak (for herself as much as others)

**Example:**
> JUDY: Okay, here's what we know. One: the flowers are toxic. Two: they're being harvested illegally. Three: I'm not letting this go. Four: I'm going to need backup. Five: that's you. Please.

**Physical Comedy:**
- Bounces when excited (literally — she's a rabbit)
- Lists on fingers (even when she runs out of fingers)
- Tries too hard — over-prepares, over-explains, over-commits
- Small physical comedy: can't reach things, gets stepped on, uses her size creatively

### The Frightened Outsider (Gary)

**Surface:** Polite, anxious, formal, eager not to offend.

**Truth:** Brave, deeply loving, desperate for belonging. His politeness is a survival mechanism; his courage emerges only when someone he loves is threatened.

**Voice Dimensions:**
| Dimension | Score | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Sarcasm | 20 | Occasionally dry, but never cruel. |
| Cynicism | 30 | Has reasons to distrust the world, chooses hope anyway. |
| Warmth | 70 | Loving, devoted, emotionally open with trusted people. |
| Formality | 90 | Full sentences, titles, no contractions when nervous. "I would be honored." |
| Verbosity | 50 | Economical when anxious, expansive when safe. |
| Subtext | 60 | Polite language masks real feelings. "I understand" often means "I am hurt." |

**Forbidden Phrases:**
- Slang or casual contractions when stressed
- Threats or disrespect
- Admitting anger directly

**Signature Lines Pattern:**
1. Formal request or observation
2. Politeness mask (apologizing for things that aren't his fault)
3. Courage emerges when stakes are personal

**Example:**
> GARY: I hope I'm not imposing. I realize this is an inconvenience. ...But if you harm my family, I will burn this building to the ground. ...I apologize for the intensity of that statement.

**Physical Comedy:**
- Coils into himself when scared (literally — he's a snake)
- Heat vision reveals truth (can't hide his emotional state)
- Cold-blooded physical gags: needs warmth, moves slowly when cold, fast when warm
- Size contrast: physically imposing but emotionally gentle

### The Meek Villain (Pawbert)

**Surface:** Helpful, timid, eager to please, slightly pathetic.

**Truth:** Desperate, resentful, chooses evil not for power but for love. Believes he has no other way to belong. The most dangerous villains are the ones who think they're the hero of their own tragedy.

**Voice Dimensions:**
| Dimension | Score | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Sarcasm | 10 | Too earnest for sarcasm. Doesn't have the confidence. |
| Cynicism | 40 | Bitter about his place in the world, but frames it as realism. |
| Warmth | 50 (fake) | Performs warmth to manipulate. Genuine warmth only for family. |
| Formality | 40 | Shifts between awkward formality and sudden, unsettling intimacy. |
| Verbosity | 60 | Rambling when nervous. Precise when explaining his plan. |
| Subtext | 80 | Never says "I want power." Says "I just want to be something." |

**Forbidden Phrases:**
- Direct cruelty without justification
- Confidence without anxiety underneath
- Admitting he's wrong (he can't — his entire identity depends on being right)

**Signature Lines Pattern:**
1. People-pleasing (deferential, apologetic)
2. Self-deprecation (earns sympathy, lowers defenses)
3. Villain monologue (suddenly articulate, passionate, tragic)

**Example:**
> PAWBERT: I know I'm not important. I know nobody thinks about me. I know I'm just... the guy who gets the coffee. But I thought — if I could just do this one thing — maybe someone would finally look at me and see... something.

**Physical Comedy:**
- Flinches from authority, then lashes out when cornered
- Defers physically — lets others walk first, sits in back, stands in shadows
- Sudden violence when pushed too far (the contrast is the horror)
- Small stature or unassuming appearance makes him invisible — which is his weapon

### The Folksy Warrior (Nibbles)

**Surface:** Uneducated, small, underestimated, speaks in simple terms.

**Truth:** Fearless, wise in ways that matter, loyal to a fault. His lack of formal education masks deep emotional intelligence and courage.

**Voice Dimensions:**
| Dimension | Score | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Sarcasm | 60 | Dry, deadpan. Often mistaken for stupidity. |
| Cynicism | 40 | Skeptical of big talkers. Judges by actions. |
| Warmth | 80 | Fiercely loyal. Loves openly once trust is earned. |
| Formality | 10 | No titles, no fancy words. Calls everyone by nicknames. |
| Verbosity | 40 | Short sentences. Says more with less. |
| Subtext | 70 | Simple words carry deep meaning. "I got you" means everything. |

**Forbidden Phrases:**
- Formal language or titles
- Self-doubt expressed aloud
- Complicated justifications

**Signature Lines Pattern:**
1. Malapropism or simple observation (underestimated)
2. Folksy wisdom (surprisingly profound)
3. Heroic action (no speech, just movement)

**Example:**
> NIBBLES: I ain't got no fancy words. I ain't got no big plan. But I know this: you don't leave family behind. Even when they're stupid. Especially when they're stupid. ...Now hold my cheese. I'm going in.

**Physical Comedy:**
- Small but fierce. Fights opponents ten times his size.
- Gnaws through obstacles (literally — he's a rodent)
- Oversized attitude in an undersized body
- Physical comedy from being underestimated: bad guys don't see him coming

## Relationship Evolution Rules

### Co-Protagonist Requirements
- Co-protagonists must have a **"catchphrase" or ritual** that evolves in meaning across the film.
  - Act 1: Casual banter ("Agree to disagree")
  - Act 2: Weaponized during conflict ("Agree to disagree — fine, disagree alone")
  - Act 3: Emotionally charged, transformed ("I don't agree to disagree. I agree to stay.")
- The partnership **must fracture at midpoint** and **reunite in the finale**.
- If the partnership never breaks, it was never tested. If it never reunites, the film is a tragedy.

### Mutual Teaching
- Each character must teach the other one **specific lesson**.
  - Judy teaches Nick: Vulnerability is not weakness. Trust is earned by giving it first.
  - Nick teaches Judy: The world is unfair — and that's why we need each other, not why we give up.
- The lessons should be demonstrated in action, not stated in dialogue, until the final act.

### Physical Contrast
- Physical contrast must be **visual and meaningful** (size, species, movement style).
- The contrast should create both comedy and thematic resonance.
- **Examples:**
  - Small prey + large predator = trust across biological fear
  - Fast + slow = different approaches to problem-solving
  - Cold-blooded + warm-blooded = different emotional temperatures
- Physical comedy arises naturally from the contrast.

## Voice Profile System

A character's voice is their fingerprint. It must be **distinct, consistent, and revealing**.

### Six Dimensions (0-100 Scale)

#### 1. Sarcasm — Irony, Understatement, Deflection
- **0:** Never ironic. Says exactly what they mean. (Judy when earnest)
- **50:** Occasionally dry. Uses irony for emphasis.
- **100:** Every sentence has a second, meaner meaning. (Nick Wilde)

#### 2. Cynicism — Negative Expectations, Distrust of Systems
- **0:** Believes in institutions, people, and happy endings. (Early Judy)
- **50:** Skeptical but hopeful. Trusts individuals, not systems.
- **100:** Expects betrayal. Plans for the worst case. (Nick Wilde)

#### 3. Warmth — Affection, Caring, Openness
- **0:** Cold, detached, emotionally unavailable.
- **50:** Cares about specific people. Polite to strangers.
- **100:** Openly loving. Hugs, compliments, emotional transparency. (Judy Hopps)

#### 4. Formality — Vocabulary, Grammar, Contractions, Titles
- **0:** Slang, fragments, nicknames only. (Nibbles)
- **50:** Mix of casual and formal. Adapts to context. (Nick Wilde in professional settings)
- **100:** Full titles, no contractions, precise vocabulary. (Gary when nervous)

#### 5. Verbosity — Word Count Per Sentence, Lists, Tangents
- **0:** Telegram speech. "No." "Won't." "Mine."
- **50:** Average length. Sometimes explains, sometimes doesn't.
- **100:** Rambling, lists, justifies every thought. (Judy Hopps)

#### 6. Subtext — Implication vs Direct Statement
- **0:** Says exactly what they feel. "I am sad." (Judy at her most vulnerable)
- **50:** Sometimes implies, sometimes states. Adapts to safety of listener.
- **100:** Never states directly. The real message is in silence, gesture, or contradiction. (Nick Wilde)

## Common Character Design Failures

1. **Same voice for all characters.** Every character sounds like the writer. Fix: Assign distinct dimension scores and forbidden phrases.
2. **Voice doesn't match archetype.** A con-man speaking like a professor. Fix: The surface and the truth must be in tension, not random.
3. **Voice doesn't evolve.** Character goes through trauma but speaks identically. Fix: Shift one dimension by 10-20 points by the finale.
4. **Voice tells instead of shows.** Character states their feelings directly. Fix: Use subtext. If they say "I'm angry," rewrite so they show it.
5. **No physical comedy.** Animation characters who don't use their bodies are wasted. Fix: Every character should have a physical signature.
6. **Relationship doesn't fracture.** Partners who never fight aren't partners — they're plot devices. Fix: Midpoint must test the relationship.
